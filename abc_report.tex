\section{Introduction}

This report summarizes an Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC) study for an adaptive-network SIR epidemic model using the scripts
\texttt{simulator.py},
\texttt{observed\_summaries.py},
\texttt{abc\_rejection.py},
\texttt{abc\_rejection\_regression.py}, and
\texttt{abc\_mcmc.py}.
We consider an SIR epidemic evolving on an adaptive contact network with $N = 200$ agents. The network changes dynamically because susceptible individuals may rewire connections away from infected neighbors, so disease transmission and network topology are coupled rather than treated as separate mechanisms.

The inferential target is the posterior distribution of the three model parameters
\[
\theta = (\beta,\gamma,\rho),
\]
where $\beta$ is the transmission probability, $\gamma$ is the recovery probability, and $\rho$ is the rewiring probability. Our goal is to infer these parameters from observed epidemic trajectories across $R = 40$ independent replicates.

The likelihood of the observed data given $\theta$ is intractable. Even a single epidemic trajectory depends on the joint probability of many correlated infection, recovery, and rewiring events occurring over time on an evolving random graph. The contact network itself is a high-dimensional latent object, and inference would require marginalizing over an enormous collection of possible network histories that are never directly observed. In practice, this makes direct likelihood-based methods such as maximum likelihood estimation or standard conjugate Bayesian analysis infeasible.

This motivates simulation-based inference, which bypasses explicit likelihood evaluation by comparing features of simulated and observed data. In this project the chosen SBI method is ABC, where simulated epidemics are accepted when their summary statistics are sufficiently close to the observed summary statistics. The two advanced methods studied beyond basic rejection ABC are regression adjustment \citep{beaumont2002} and ABC-MCMC \citep{marjoram2003}.

The simulator in \texttt{simulator.py} uses an adaptive contact network with an Erd\H{o}s--R\'enyi initial graph of edge probability $p_{\text{edge}}=0.05$, $5$ initially infected individuals, and a horizon of $T=200$ discrete time steps. At each step the model applies infection, recovery, and network rewiring, so the network co-evolves with the epidemic throughout the simulation.

The script \texttt{observed\_summaries.py} aggregates $40$ observed replicates into six summary statistics, which are then matched against simulated summaries inside the ABC procedures. Details regarding the choice of summary statistics are provided in Table~\ref{tab:summary-statistics-role}. The observed summaries used in this run are listed in Table~\ref{tab:observed-summaries}.

More precisely, let $Y^{(r)}$ denote the data from replicate $r \in \{1,\dots,R\}$, and let $\eta_j(\cdot)$ denote the $j$th replicate-level summary functional. The implementation first computes the summary within each replicate and then averages across replicates, so the observed summary fed into ABC is
\[
\widehat{s}^{\,\mathrm{obs}}_j
= \frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R} \eta_j\!\left(Y^{(r)}\right),
\qquad j=1,\dots,6.
\]
In statistical terms, $\widehat{s}^{\,\mathrm{obs}}_j$ is the empirical mean of the replicate-level summaries and is used as an estimator of the population quantity
\[
s_j^{\mathrm{obs}} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\eta_j(Y)\right].
\]
Under the assumption that the $R=40$ observed replicates are independent realizations from the same underlying epidemic mechanism, the sample average above is a natural estimator of this expectation and converges to $\mathbb{E}[\eta_j(Y)]$ by the law of large numbers.

This is exactly how \texttt{observed\_summaries.py} is written. For example, the observed maximum infection fraction is computed as
\[
\widehat{s}^{\,\mathrm{obs}}_1
= \frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R} \max_t I_t^{(r)},
\]
the observed time to peak is
\[
\widehat{s}^{\,\mathrm{obs}}_2
= \frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R} \arg\max_t I_t^{(r)},
\]
and the same averaging principle is applied to the early-growth slopes, rewiring slopes, per-replicate degree variances, and late-decay slopes. Hence the values in Table~\ref{tab:observed-summaries} should be interpreted as empirical approximations to expected summary statistics, rather than as summaries from a single trajectory.

The ABC workflow follows a standard simulation-based inference pattern. Parameter draws are sampled from the priors
\[
\beta \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0.05,0.50), \qquad
\gamma \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0.02,0.20), \qquad
\rho \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0,0.80),
\]
the epidemic is simulated, the six summaries are computed, and the simulated summaries are compared with the observed summaries after standardization. This report first analyzes the basic ABC rejection results, then studies two extensions that move beyond the raw rejection posterior: regression adjustment and ABC-MCMC.

\section{Basic ABC Rejection}

\subsection{Algorithm}

The baseline algorithm is implemented in \texttt{abc\_rejection.py}. It follows the standard rejection-ABC logic of simulating from the prior and retaining draws whose summaries lie close to the observed summaries \citep{beaumont2002}. The script performs $N_{\text{sim}}=30{,}000$ prior simulations and stores the simulated parameter vector and corresponding six-dimensional summary vector from each run. Because the summaries are on very different scales, the script standardizes them using the simulation mean and standard deviation before computing distances. If $s_i$ denotes the simulated summary vector and $s_{\text{obs}}$ denotes the observed summary vector, then the distance used in the script is
\[
d_i = \left\lVert \tilde{s}_i - \tilde{s}_{\text{obs}} \right\rVert_2,
\]
where tildes indicate the simulation-based standardization.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:abc-rejection} summarizes the implementation-level workflow in \texttt{abc\_rejection.py}. The pseudocode mirrors the actual script structure: repeated simulation under independent random seeds, simulation-based standardization, quantile-based acceptance for several $\epsilon$ values, and an additional Reduced set E calibration export for later methods.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Basic ABC rejection pipeline used in \texttt{abc\_rejection.py}}
\label{alg:abc-rejection}
\Proc{\textsc{OneSimulation}$(u,\mathcal{C})$}{
    sample $\beta \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0.05,0.50)$, $\gamma \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0.02,0.20)$, $\rho \sim \mathrm{Unif}(0,0.80)$\;
    $(I_{0:T}, W_{0:T}, H) \gets \texttt{simulate}(\beta,\gamma,\rho;\, \texttt{rng}=u,\texttt{simulation\_context}=\mathcal{C})$\;
    $s_1 \gets \max_t I_t$\;
    $s_2 \gets \arg\max_t I_t$\;
    $s_3 \gets \text{linear slope of } \log(I_t)$ on $t=2,\dots,6$\;
    $s_4 \gets \text{linear slope of } \log(W_t)$ on $t=2,\dots,6$\;
    $s_5 \gets \text{variance implied by final degree histogram } H$\;
    $s_6 \gets \text{linear slope of } \log(I_t)$ on $t=13,\dots,20$\;
    \KwRet $(s,\theta)$ where $s=(s_1,\dots,s_6)$ and $\theta=(\beta,\gamma,\rho)$\;
}
\BlankLine
\Proc{\textsc{ABCRejection}$(N_{\mathrm{sim}}, \mathcal{E})$}{
    generate $N_{\mathrm{sim}}$ independent simulation seeds from \texttt{SeedSequence}(2026)\;
    $\mathcal{C} \gets \texttt{BuildSimulationContext}(N)$\;
    \For{each simulation seed $u_i$}{
        $(s_i,\theta_i) \gets \textsc{OneSimulation}(u_i,\mathcal{C})$\;
    }
    $s_{\mathrm{obs}} \gets \texttt{GetObservedSummaries}()$\;
    standardize $\{s_i\}_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{sim}}}$ and $s_{\mathrm{obs}}$ using the simulation mean and standard deviation\;
    compute distances $d_i = \lVert \tilde{s}_i - \tilde{s}_{\mathrm{obs}} \rVert_2$\;
    $\tau_{\epsilon} \gets \epsilon$-quantile of distances $\{d_i\}$\;
    $A_{\epsilon} \gets \{i : d_i \le \tau_{\epsilon}\}$\;
    Accepted parameters $\gets \{\theta_i : i \in A_{\epsilon}\}$ (save to CSV)\;
}
\end{algorithm}

For a chosen acceptance proportion $\epsilon$, the rejection sampler sets the tolerance to the empirical $\epsilon$-quantile of the simulated distances. Equivalently, a draw is accepted if
\[
d_i \le q_{\epsilon},
\]
where $q_{\epsilon}$ is the empirical quantile of the distance distribution. This is a convenient way to control the posterior sample size directly. In the present run, the script experiments with
\[
\epsilon \in \{0.005,\; 0.01,\; 0.03\},
\]
so the accepted sample sizes are exactly $150$, $300$, and $900$ respectively.

The six summaries were chosen to cover both epidemic shape and network adaptation. Their roles in the inference procedure are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:summary-statistics-role}.

\subsection{Epsilon Experimentation}

The tolerance experiment in \texttt{abc\_rejection.py} compares the posterior under three acceptance levels. The summary-overlay plots produced in
\texttt{outputs/basic\_abc/sanity\_check/}
show the expected bias--variance trade-off: smaller $\epsilon$ values force the accepted summaries to sit closer to the observed data, while larger $\epsilon$ values admit many more draws and therefore produce smoother but more weakly targeted posteriors. This pattern is especially visible in the generated overlays for maximum infection fraction and late infection decay.

Table~\ref{tab:epsilon-results} shows the posterior summaries for the rich six-summary specification. The posterior for $\gamma$ is more stable, but it still drifts slightly upward as the acceptance region becomes looser.

\section{Extensions Beyond ABC-Rejection Posteriors}

The methods in this section are motivated by the SBI literature on improving efficiency or reducing approximation bias beyond basic rejection ABC. In particular, regression adjustment follows the local-linear correction idea of \citet{beaumont2002}, while ABC-MCMC follows the likelihood-free MCMC construction of \citet{marjoram2003}.

\subsection{Regression Adjusted}

The script \texttt{abc\_rejection\_regression.py} applies the Beaumont-style local linear adjustment to the accepted rejection samples from Reduced set E at $\epsilon=0.01$ \citep{beaumont2002}. If $\theta_i$ is an accepted parameter draw and $s_i$ is its accepted summary vector, the adjustment is of the form
\[
\theta_i^{\text{adj}} = \theta_i - (s_i - s_{\text{obs}})^\top \widehat{b},
\]
where $\widehat{b}$ is estimated by weighted linear regression of parameters on accepted summaries. The weights are Epanechnikov-style kernel weights based on the accepted distances:
\[
w_i = 1 - \left(\frac{d_i}{h}\right)^2,
\]
with $h$ equal to the largest accepted distance in the reference rejection sample. Intuitively, this tries to remove the local linear bias that remains because the accepted summaries are close to, but not exactly equal to, the observed summaries.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:regression-adjustment} reproduces the implementation in \texttt{abc\_rejection\_regression.py}, including the use of the maximum accepted distance as the kernel bandwidth and a separate weighted linear regression for each parameter.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Regression-adjusted ABC used in \texttt{abc\_rejection\_regression.py}}
\label{alg:regression-adjustment}
\Proc{\textsc{RegressionAdjustedABC}$(\Theta_{\mathrm{acc}}, S_{\mathrm{acc}}, s_{\mathrm{obs}}, d_{\mathrm{acc}})$}{
    initialize $\Theta_{\mathrm{adj}}$ as a zero matrix with the same shape as $\Theta_{\mathrm{acc}}$\;
    $h \gets \max(d_{\mathrm{acc}})$\;
    \For{each accepted sample index $i$}{
        $w_i \gets 1 - (d_{\mathrm{acc},i}/h)^2$\;
        set $w_i \gets 0$ if $d_{\mathrm{acc},i} > h$\;
    }
    \For{each parameter coordinate $j \in \{\beta,\gamma,\rho\}$}{
        fit weighted linear regression of $\Theta_{\mathrm{acc}}[:,j]$ on $S_{\mathrm{acc}}$ using weights $\{w_i\}$\;
        let $\widehat{b}_j$ be the fitted coefficient vector\;
        $\Theta_{\mathrm{adj}}[:,j] \gets \Theta_{\mathrm{acc}}[:,j] - (S_{\mathrm{acc}} - s_{\mathrm{obs}})\widehat{b}_j$\;
    }
    save before-versus-after posterior histograms\;
    save $\Theta_{\mathrm{adj}}$ to \texttt{abc\_regression\_adjusted\_output.csv}\;
    \KwRet $\Theta_{\mathrm{adj}}$\;
}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{ABC-MCMC}

The final extension is implemented in \texttt{abc\_mcmc.py}. It follows the basic idea of performing MCMC while replacing the unavailable likelihood evaluation with an ABC acceptance rule \citep{marjoram2003}. Rather than recalibrating the problem from scratch, the script loads the Reduced set E rejection output from \texttt{data/intermediate/abc\_rejection\_output.npz}. This ensures that the summary subset, standardization constants, observed summary vector, and acceptance threshold are exactly the same as in the reference rejection run, making the comparison fair.

The proposal mechanism is a Gaussian random walk with componentwise scales
\[
(0.035,\ 0.012,\ 0.06)
\]
for $(\beta,\gamma,\rho)$ respectively, constrained to remain within the original uniform prior support. The script runs $30{,}000$ proposals, stores $30{,}001$ chain states including the initial accepted state, discards the first $3{,}000$ iterations as burn-in, and retains $27{,}000$ posterior samples. The fixed ABC distance threshold loaded from the rejection calibration is
\[
\tau = 0.401377.
\]

Algorithm~\ref{alg:abc-mcmc} summarizes the actual ABC-MCMC routine in \texttt{abc\_mcmc.py}. The key implementation detail is that, because the proposal is symmetric and the prior is uniform on a rectangular support, proposals are accepted exactly when they remain in prior support and their simulated Reduced set E summary is within the fixed rejection-calibrated threshold.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{ABC-MCMC with fixed rejection calibration used in \texttt{abc\_mcmc.py}}
\label{alg:abc-mcmc}
\Proc{\textsc{ABCMCMC}$(\theta^{(0)}, s^{(0)}, d^{(0)}, \tau)$}{
    initialize the chain at $\theta^{(0)} = \texttt{initial\_parameters}$, $s^{(0)} = \texttt{initial\_summary}$, and $d^{(0)} = \texttt{initial\_distance}$\;
    \For{$t \gets 1$ \KwTo $N_{\mathrm{mcmc}} - 1$}{
        propose $\theta' \gets \theta^{(t-1)} + \mathcal{N}(0,\mathrm{diag}(0.035,0.012,0.06)^2)$\;
        \If{$\theta'$ lies inside the prior box}{
            simulate the full six-summary vector under $\theta'$\;
            keep the Reduced set E entries $(s_1,s_2,s_6,s_4,s_5)$ only\;
            standardize the proposed summary with the saved rejection scaling terms\;
            compute $d' = \lVert \tilde{s}' - \tilde{s}_{\mathrm{obs}} \rVert_2$\;
            \If{$d'$ is finite \KwAnd $d' \le \tau$}{
                accept the proposal:
                $\theta^{(t)} \gets \theta'$,
                $s^{(t)} \gets s'$,
                $d^{(t)} \gets d'$\;
            }
            \Else{
                keep the previous state\;
            }
        }
        \Else{
            keep the previous state\;
        }
        store the current chain state, summary, distance, and acceptance indicator\;
    }
    discard the first $3000$ stored states and apply thinning factor $1$\;
    save posterior samples, diagnostics, trace plots, and rejection-versus-MCMC comparison plots\;
}
\end{algorithm}

The MCMC traces do not show obvious divergence or runaway behavior. They exhibit persistent local dependence, which is expected for a random-walk chain, but they still explore the same high-density regions found by rejection ABC. This is consistent with the modest but workable acceptance rate of about $12.7\%$.

The posterior means from the retained ABC-MCMC sample are
\[
(0.1681,\ 0.0872,\ 0.2762),
\]
with empirical 95\% posterior intervals
\[
\beta \in [0.0995,\ 0.2798], \qquad
\gamma \in [0.0533,\ 0.1217], \qquad
\rho \in [0.0284,\ 0.5466].
\]
These are extremely close to the Reduced set E rejection posterior
\[
(0.1650,\ 0.0861,\ 0.2740),
\]
which suggests that the basic rejection sample already captures the main posterior mass well. The practical advantage of ABC-MCMC is therefore not that it radically changes the answer, but that it provides a much larger posterior sample under the same ABC target while preserving the same inferential conclusions.

Overall, the scripts and saved plots tell a coherent story. The baseline rejection ABC posterior is already informative, $\epsilon=0.01$ gives a reasonable balance between fit and sample size, Reduced set E is the most faithful reduced-summary approximation to the rich-set posterior, regression adjustment offers a mild bias correction, and ABC-MCMC confirms the same posterior structure with a much larger retained sample.

\subsection{Diagnostic Discussion of the Epsilon Choice}
\label{app:epsilon-diagnostics}

This appendix gives a more detailed explanation of why $\epsilon = 0.01$ was chosen as the main operating tolerance in the rejection ABC analysis. The key evidence comes from the six diagnostic summary overlays saved in \texttt{outputs/basic\_abc/sanity\_check/}, where the accepted simulated summaries for $\epsilon \in \{0.005,0.01,0.03\}$ are plotted against the observed value shown by the \textcolor{red}{red vertical line}.

For each panel, the \textcolor{red}{red vertical line} represents the calculated mean summary statistic from the observed data, namely
\[
\widehat{s}^{\,\mathrm{obs}}_j = \frac{1}{R}\sum_{r=1}^{R}\eta_j\!\left(Y^{(r)}\right),
\qquad R = 40.
\]
This is the same observed summary value used in the ABC distance calculation. Hence the diagnostic plots should be read as a comparison between the distribution of accepted simulated summaries and the empirical mean summary extracted from the observed replicates.

Across the six summaries, the strictest tolerance $\epsilon = 0.005$ usually gives the tightest match to the observed summaries, but it uses only $150$ accepted draws and therefore produces visibly rougher histograms. At the other extreme, $\epsilon = 0.03$ yields $900$ accepted draws, but this broader acceptance region admits many simulations whose summaries lie further from the observed line, resulting in posterior samples of higher variance. The intermediate choice $\epsilon = 0.01$ provides a better compromise: the accepted histograms remain centered near the observed summaries while still retaining $300$ posterior draws for more stable inference.

Figures~\ref{fig:appendix-epsilon-diagnostics-1}--\ref{fig:appendix-epsilon-diagnostics-3} show the full set of diagnostic plots. For maximum infection fraction and time to peak, the overlays indicate that $\epsilon = 0.01$ retains a close match to the observed epidemic peak behavior while avoiding the extra spread seen at $\epsilon = 0.03$. For early infection growth and early rewiring growth, the same pattern holds, but the gain from moving from $\epsilon = 0.005$ to $\epsilon = 0.01$ is especially useful because the histogram becomes less noisy without a clear loss of fit.

The degree-variance and late-decay diagnostics are particularly informative for the rewiring component $\rho$. The degree-variance plot is especially useful for defending the move from $\epsilon = 0.005$ to $\epsilon = 0.01$: when the tolerance is relaxed from $0.005$ to $0.01$, the accepted histogram becomes less ragged because the sample size increases from $150$ to $300$, yet it remains centered close to the \textcolor{red}{red reference line}. In other words, the increase in $\epsilon$ produces a more stable approximation without introducing a substantial loss of calibration for this summary. Once $\epsilon$ is increased further to $0.03$, however, the accepted distribution becomes noticeably more spread out around the observed mean, which is consistent with a higher-variance posterior and weaker calibration for the network-adaptation aspect of the model. This is one of the clearest visual arguments for preferring $\epsilon = 0.01$ over both the noisier $\epsilon = 0.005$ fit and the more weakly targeted $\epsilon = 0.03$ fit.
